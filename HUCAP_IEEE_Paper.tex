\begin{abstract}
Accurate prediction of protein functions from amino acid sequences is a cornerstone of computational biology, yet modern deep learning models frequently produce overconfident predictions that can mislead downstream analysis in drug discovery and functional genomics. We present HUCAP (Hybrid Uncertainty-Calibrated Protein Prediction), a framework that integrates uncertainty quantification, post-hoc calibration, and explainable AI into a unified pipeline built upon the ESM2 protein language model. Trained and evaluated on a curated dataset of 46{,}978 proteins annotated with approximately 400 Gene Ontology terms from CAFA-6 and GOA sources, HUCAP introduces three key contributions: (1) a hybrid uncertainty scoring mechanism combining Shannon entropy and $k$-mer diversity for data-driven complexity classification, (2) a multi-stage calibration pipeline employing Temperature Scaling and Isotonic Regression that reduces Expected Calibration Error (ECE) by 78.6\% without degrading discriminative performance ($F_{\max}=0.3943$), and (3) a novel Uncertainty-Adjusted Confidence (UAC) metric with confidence-based fail-safe rejection that algorithmically withholds unreliable predictions. Evaluation on CAFA-compliant benchmarks demonstrates that HUCAP achieves calibration quality of ECE = 0.0560 and Brier = 0.0835 while producing Micro~F1 = 0.58 and Macro~F1 = 0.42. Ablation studies confirm the individual contribution of each component, and statistical tests (Wilcoxon signed-rank, paired $t$-test, bootstrap CI) establish significance at $p < 10^{-99}$ with Cohen's $d = 5.40$. To the best of our knowledge, HUCAP is the first framework to jointly address uncertainty estimation, post-hoc calibration, confidence-based rejection, and quantitative explainability for multi-aspect Gene Ontology protein function prediction.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

Protein function prediction from amino acid sequences is a fundamental challenge in computational biology, with direct applications in drug discovery~\cite{lin2023}, functional genomics, and disease mechanism elucidation~\cite{radivojac2013}. The Critical Assessment of Protein Function Annotation (CAFA) challenges have established standardized benchmarks that frame this task as multi-label classification over Gene Ontology (GO) terms across three aspects: Molecular Function (MF), Biological Process (BP), and Cellular Component (CC).

Recent advances in protein language models (pLMs), particularly the ESM family~\cite{rives2021, lin2023}, have demonstrated remarkable capability in learning contextual representations from evolutionary information encoded in protein sequences. However, these transformer-based models inherit a critical limitation shared across modern deep learning: \textbf{systematic overconfidence} in their probabilistic outputs~\cite{guo2017}. A model that assigns 90\% confidence to a prediction it gets right only 60\% of the time poses a serious risk in safety-critical biological applications, where researchers may base experimental decisions on predicted confidence scores.

This calibration gap is particularly problematic in protein function prediction for several reasons: (i) the label space is vast and hierarchically structured, (ii) severe class imbalance is inherent, and (iii) intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) and low-complexity sequences fall outside the training distribution of most pLMs, yet receive high-confidence predictions nonetheless. Despite these challenges, existing protein function prediction systems rarely incorporate formal uncertainty estimation or calibration.

To address these limitations, we propose \textbf{HUCAP} (Hybrid Uncertainty-Calibrated Protein Prediction), an end-to-end framework that systematically addresses prediction reliability through four interconnected mechanisms:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hybrid Uncertainty Quantification}: A dual-signal complexity analysis combining Shannon entropy (global compositional bias) and $k$-mer diversity (local sequential repetitiveness) with data-driven, percentile-calibrated classification thresholds.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Stage Calibration}: A post-hoc calibration pipeline integrating Temperature Scaling~\cite{guo2017} and Isotonic Regression~\cite{zadrozny2002} that reduces ECE by 78.6\% while preserving discriminative ranking.
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty-Adjusted Confidence (UAC)}: A novel ranking metric that harmonizes calibrated probabilities with epistemic uncertainty penalties, ensuring high-uncertainty predictions are algorithmically down-ranked.
    \item \textbf{Confidence-Based Rejection with Quantitative XAI}: A fail-safe system that withholds predictions when joint uncertainty and low-confidence criteria are met, accompanied by step-by-step confidence decomposition for full transparency.
\end{enumerate}

To the best of our knowledge, no existing protein function prediction system jointly integrates input-level uncertainty estimation, post-hoc output calibration, a unified confidence ranking metric, and explainable confidence decomposition within a single deployable pipeline.

\section{Related Work}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Protein Language Models}
The evolution from feature-engineered approaches to transformer-based protein language models has fundamentally transformed protein function prediction. ESM-1b~\cite{rives2021} demonstrated that self-supervised pre-training on millions of protein sequences yields representations competitive with alignment-based methods. ESM2~\cite{lin2023} further improved these representations through architectural advances, achieving state-of-the-art performance across multiple structural and functional benchmarks. ProtTrans~\cite{elnaggar2021} explored various transformer architectures (BERT, Albert, Electra, XLNet) for protein representation learning. While these models exhibit strong discriminative capability, none systematically address calibration or uncertainty in their predictions.

\subsection{Uncertainty Estimation in Deep Learning}
Uncertainty estimation for neural networks has been studied from both Bayesian and frequentist perspectives. MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016} interprets dropout as approximate Bayesian inference, enabling uncertainty estimation through multiple stochastic forward passes. Deep ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017} aggregate predictions from independently trained models. Evidential deep learning~\cite{sensoy2018} directly predicts Dirichlet parameters over class probabilities. However, these methods have seen limited adoption in protein function prediction, where the multi-label, multi-aspect prediction structure introduces unique challenges for uncertainty characterization.

\subsection{Calibration Methods}
Guo~et~al.~\cite{guo2017} demonstrated that modern neural networks are poorly calibrated and proposed Temperature Scaling---a single-parameter post-hoc method that rescales logits. Isotonic Regression~\cite{zadrozny2002} provides a non-parametric alternative capable of achieving near-perfect calibration on validation data. Platt Scaling~\cite{platt1999} fits a logistic regression on model outputs. While calibration has been extensively studied in computer vision and NLP, its application to protein function prediction remains largely unexplored. Existing protein prediction systems report discriminative metrics ($F_{\max}$, AUPRC) but rarely assess or report calibration quality.

\subsection{Gaps Addressed by HUCAP}
To the best of our knowledge, no existing protein function prediction framework jointly integrates: (i) input-level uncertainty estimation based on sequence composition, (ii) post-hoc output calibration, (iii) a unified confidence metric bridging both signals, and (iv) explainable confidence decomposition. HUCAP addresses all four gaps within a single, deployable pipeline.

\section{Methodology}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Given a protein sequence $S = (s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_L)$ where $s_i \in \mathcal{A}$ (the alphabet of 20 standard amino acids) and $L$ is the sequence length, the goal is to predict a set of Gene Ontology (GO) terms $\mathcal{Y} \subseteq \mathcal{G}$ with calibrated confidence scores. We decompose this into three sub-problems corresponding to the GO aspects: Molecular Function ($\mathcal{G}^F$), Biological Process ($\mathcal{G}^P$), and Cellular Component ($\mathcal{G}^C$).

\subsection{Feature Extraction and Fusion Architecture}

\subsubsection{ESM2 Encoder}
We employ the ESM2-t12-35M model~\cite{lin2023} as the backbone encoder. To leverage the pre-trained evolutionary representations while maintaining computational efficiency on our 46K-protein dataset, the ESM2 backbone is kept frozen during training. For a tokenized input sequence, the encoder produces contextual embeddings:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{H} = \text{ESM2}(s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_L) \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}
\end{equation}
where $d = 480$ is the hidden dimension. Mean pooling over non-padding tokens yields the sequence representation $\mathbf{h}_{\text{esm}} \in \mathbb{R}^d$.

\subsubsection{Multi-Source Feature Fusion}
We augment ESM2 embeddings with two complementary feature sources:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PSSM features} $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$: Position-Specific Scoring Matrix profiles capturing evolutionary conservation across 20 amino acid types.
    \item \textbf{Physicochemical features} $\mathbf{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{3}$: Molecular weight, hydrophobicity, and charge properties encoding structural physics.
\end{itemize}

Rather than simple concatenation, we employ a \textit{structured attention-based fusion} mechanism. Each feature source is projected to a shared $d$-dimensional space via learned linear transformations:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{W}_i \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{b}_i, \quad i \in \{\text{esm}, \text{pssm}, \text{phys}\}
\end{equation}

The projected features are stacked into a matrix $\mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times d}$, and attention weights are computed via a learned context vector $\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^d$:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_i = \frac{\exp(\tanh(\mathbf{e}_i)^T \mathbf{q})}{\sum_{j=1}^{3} \exp(\tanh(\mathbf{e}_j)^T \mathbf{q})}
\end{equation}

The fused context vector is obtained as the weighted sum plus a residual connection to the original ESM2 embedding:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z} = \text{LayerNorm}\left(\text{GELU}\left(\sum_{i=1}^{3} \alpha_i \mathbf{e}_i\right)\right) + \mathbf{h}_{\text{esm}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Multi-Task Classification Heads}
The fused representation $\mathbf{z}$ feeds into three independent classification heads (one per GO aspect), each consisting of a two-layer MLP with LayerNorm, GELU activation, and dropout:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{y}}^a = \sigma(\mathbf{W}_2^a \cdot \text{GELU}(\text{LN}(\mathbf{W}_1^a \mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}_1^a)) + \mathbf{b}_2^a)
\end{equation}
where $a \in \{F, P, C\}$ denotes the GO aspect and $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function applied element-wise.

\subsection{Uncertainty Estimation}

We introduce a hybrid uncertainty scoring mechanism that operates directly on the input sequence, complementing model-level uncertainty.

\subsubsection{Shannon Entropy}
For a sequence $S$ with amino acid frequency distribution $\{p_1, \ldots, p_{20}\}$, the Shannon entropy quantifies global compositional diversity:
\begin{equation}
    H(S) = -\sum_{i=1}^{20} p(a_i) \log_2 p(a_i)
    \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}
where $p(a_i) = \frac{\text{count}(a_i)}{L}$. The theoretical maximum is $H_{\max} = \log_2(20) \approx 4.32$ bits for a uniform amino acid distribution. Sequences with low entropy (e.g., polyglutamine tracts, proline-rich regions) exhibit compositional bias that places them outside the ESM2 pre-training distribution (UniRef50), making predictions on such sequences inherently unreliable. Entropy thus serves as a \textit{proxy for epistemic uncertainty}.

\subsubsection{k-mer Diversity}
While entropy captures global compositional bias, $k$-mer diversity measures local sequential repetitiveness:
\begin{equation}
    D_k(S) = \frac{|\{S[i:i+k] \mid 0 \leq i \leq L-k\}|}{L - k + 1}
    \label{eq:kmer}
\end{equation}
This metric resolves cases where entropy alone is insufficient. For example, the sequence ``\texttt{ACACAC...}'' has moderate entropy (two distinct residues) but very low 3-mer diversity (only ``ACA'' and ``CAC''), indicating tandem repeats that the model may mishandle.

\subsubsection{Hybrid Complexity Score}
We combine normalized entropy and $k$-mer diversity into a unified complexity score:
\begin{equation}
    C(S) = w_e \cdot \frac{H(S)}{H_{\max}} + w_k \cdot D_k(S)
    \label{eq:complexity}
\end{equation}
where $w_e = 0.7$ and $w_k = 0.3$ are empirically tuned weights that prioritize the information-theoretic signal while retaining sensitivity to repetitive motifs.

\subsection{Sequence Complexity Classification}

Sequences are classified into three tiers using data-driven thresholds derived from the training set entropy distribution (computed over the full 46{,}978-protein dataset):

\begin{equation}
    \text{tier}(S) = \begin{cases}
        \textsc{Low Complexity} & \text{if } H(S) < \tau_{\text{low}} \\
        \textsc{Medium Complexity} & \text{if } \tau_{\text{low}} \leq H(S) < \tau_{\text{med}} \\
        \textsc{Structured} & \text{if } H(S) \geq \tau_{\text{med}}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{\text{low}} = 3.80$ (10th percentile) and $\tau_{\text{med}} = 3.85$ (30th percentile) are calibrated from the training set. These thresholds are dataset-dependent and are automatically recomputed when the training distribution changes, ensuring that the classification adapts to the scale and composition of the data. The full algorithm is detailed in Algorithm~\ref{alg:complexity}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Complexity Classification}
\label{alg:complexity}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Protein sequence $S$, thresholds $\tau_{\text{low}}, \tau_{\text{med}}$
\Ensure Sequence type, uncertainty level, entropy $H$, complexity $C$
\State $H \gets$ \Call{ShannonEntropy}{$S$} \Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:entropy}}
\State $D \gets$ \Call{KmerDiversity}{$S, k{=}3$} \Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:kmer}}
\State $C \gets 0.7 \cdot \frac{H}{H_{\max}} + 0.3 \cdot D$ \Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:complexity}}
\If{$H < \tau_{\text{low}}$}
    \State \Return (\texttt{low\_complexity}, \texttt{HIGH}, $H$, $C$)
\ElsIf{$H < \tau_{\text{med}}$}
    \State \Return (\texttt{medium\_complexity}, \texttt{MEDIUM}, $H$, $C$)
\Else
    \State \Return (\texttt{structured}, \texttt{LOW}, $H$, $C$)
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Post-Hoc Calibration Pipeline}

Modern neural networks optimized with cross-entropy loss tend to produce overconfident predictions~\cite{guo2017}. HUCAP addresses this through a two-stage post-hoc calibration pipeline applied after training, preserving the discriminative ranking of the base model.

\subsubsection{Temperature Scaling}
A single scalar parameter $T > 0$ is learned on a held-out validation set by minimizing the negative log-likelihood:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{p} = \sigma(z / T)
    \label{eq:temp}
\end{equation}
where $z$ denotes the raw logit. When $T > 1$, probabilities are softened (more conservative predictions, reduced overconfidence). When $T < 1$, probabilities are sharpened. The key theoretical advantage is that Temperature Scaling preserves the \textit{argmax} ordering---the same predictions are ranked identically, but their magnitude is calibrated.

\subsubsection{Isotonic Regression}
As a second calibration stage, Isotonic Regression~\cite{zadrozny2002} learns a non-parametric, monotonically non-decreasing mapping $g: [0,1] \to [0,1]$ from temperature-scaled probabilities to empirical frequencies:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{cal}} = g(\hat{p})
\end{equation}
This step achieves near-perfect calibration on validation data (ECE $\approx 0$) while maintaining monotonicity.

The complete calibration pipeline is formalized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:calibration}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Multi-Stage Calibration Pipeline}
\label{alg:calibration}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Raw logits $\mathbf{z}$, optimal temperature $T^*$, isotonic model $g$
\Ensure Calibrated probability $p_{\text{cal}}$
\State $\hat{p} \gets \sigma(\mathbf{z} / T^*)$ \Comment{Temperature Scaling}
\State $p_{\text{cal}} \gets g(\hat{p})$ \Comment{Isotonic Regression}
\State \textbf{assert} $\text{rank}(p_{\text{cal}}) = \text{rank}(\hat{p})$ \Comment{Ranking preserved}
\State \Return $p_{\text{cal}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Smooth Confidence Scaling}

For sequences classified as non-structured, we apply a smooth, entropy-proportional confidence adjustment that replaces naive hard penalty multipliers:
\begin{equation}
    c_{\text{adj}} = c_{\text{raw}} \cdot \max\!\left(\frac{H(S)}{H_{\max}},\; c_{\text{floor}}\right)
    \label{eq:smooth}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\text{floor}} = 0.30$ prevents over-penalization. This design offers three advantages over binary penalty schemes: (i) \textit{continuity}---no artificial boundary effects, (ii) \textit{proportionality}---the penalty scales with actual compositional bias severity, and (iii) \textit{safety floor}---predictions are never reduced to negligible values.

\subsection{Uncertainty-Adjusted Confidence (UAC)}

We introduce the Uncertainty-Adjusted Confidence (UAC) as the definitive ranking metric that harmonizes post-hoc calibration with epistemic uncertainty penalties:
\begin{equation}
    \text{UAC} = p_{\text{cal}} \cdot \left(1.0 - \frac{P_{\text{uncert}}}{2}\right)
    \label{eq:uac}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\text{uncert}} = 1 - \frac{H(S)}{H_{\max}}$ is the normalized uncertainty penalty. The division by 2 ensures that strong biological signals from the model are never completely erased by uncertainty penalization, preserving discriminative power while enforcing safety.

The UAC serves as the primary prediction ranking score, replacing raw probability-based ordering. Its theoretical advantages include: (a) high-uncertainty predictions are algorithmically down-ranked even if the model is internally confident, preventing overconfident false discoveries; (b) the half-scaling of the penalty ensures extremely strong true signals survive penalization; (c) it provides a single, interpretable metric reflecting true prediction reliability.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{UAC Computation}
\label{alg:uac}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Calibrated probability $p_{\text{cal}}$, sequence entropy $H(S)$, maximum entropy $H_{\max}$
\Ensure Uncertainty-Adjusted Confidence score
\State $\hat{H} \gets \min(H(S) / H_{\max},\; 1.0)$ \Comment{Normalize}
\State $P_{\text{uncert}} \gets 1.0 - \hat{H}$ \Comment{Uncertainty penalty}
\State $\text{scale} \gets 1.0 - (P_{\text{uncert}} \times 0.5)$ \Comment{Half-penalize}
\State $\text{UAC} \gets p_{\text{cal}} \times \text{scale}$
\State \Return $\text{UAC}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Confidence-Based Rejection System}

To prevent unreliable predictions from reaching downstream analysis, HUCAP implements a fail-safe rejection mechanism that withholds predictions when two conditions are jointly satisfied:
\begin{equation}
    \text{reject}(S) = \begin{cases}
        \text{True} & \text{if } \text{tier}(S) = \textsc{High} \;\wedge\; \max_i(\text{UAC}_i) < \theta_r \\
        \text{False} & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_r = 0.45$ is the rejection threshold. This dual-criterion design ensures that rejection occurs only when both sequence-level uncertainty and model-level confidence corroborate the prediction's unreliability.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Fail-Safe Rejection System}
\label{alg:reject}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Predictions $\mathcal{P}$, uncertainty level $U$, threshold $\theta_r = 0.45$
\Ensure Accept or reject decision with explanation
\State $c_{\max} \gets \max_{p \in \mathcal{P}} p.\text{scaled\_confidence}$
\If{$U = \texttt{HIGH}$ \textbf{and} $c_{\max} < \theta_r$}
    \State $\text{withheld} \gets \texttt{True}$
    \State Generate structured rejection explanation
\Else
    \State $\text{withheld} \gets \texttt{False}$
    \State Rank predictions by UAC, apply tier-based capping
    \If{$U = \texttt{HIGH}$}
        \State Cap results to top-2 predictions
    \ElsIf{$U = \texttt{MEDIUM}$}
        \State Cap results to top-3 predictions
    \EndIf
\EndIf
\State \Return $(\text{withheld}, \mathcal{P}_{\text{ranked}})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Explainable Confidence Decomposition}

HUCAP provides quantitative transparency through a step-by-step decomposition of the final UAC score into interpretable components:
\begin{equation}
    \text{UAC} = \underbrace{p_{\text{base}}}_{\text{Raw}} + \underbrace{(p_{\text{cal}} - p_{\text{base}})}_{\text{Calibration}} - \underbrace{\Delta_E}_{\text{Entropy}} - \underbrace{\Delta_D}_{\text{Diversity}}
    \label{eq:decomp}
\end{equation}

This additive decomposition enables researchers to identify \textit{exactly why} a prediction was deemed uncertain. For instance, a decomposition report might indicate: ``base probability 0.72, calibration reduced by 0.08, entropy penalty of 0.12 applied due to low compositional diversity (entropy = 2.1 bits).'' Additionally, HUCAP generates counterfactual explanations (e.g., ``if the sequence entropy increased to 3.85 bits, the penalty would be removed'') to guide experimental interpretation.

\subsection{Token-Level Attribution}

For local explainability, HUCAP extracts attention-based token attributions from the final transformer layer. The importance of each residue $s_i$ is computed as the average attention weight from the \texttt{[CLS]} token across all heads:
\begin{equation}
    \text{importance}(s_i) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{H}|} \sum_{h \in \mathcal{H}} A^{(h)}_{\texttt{CLS}, i}
\end{equation}
where $A^{(h)}$ is the attention matrix of head $h$ in the final layer. These normalized scores are presented as an interactive residue importance map in the web interface.

\section{Experimental Setup}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Dataset}
We use the CAFA-6 protein function prediction benchmark dataset, comprising protein sequences annotated with GO terms from the Gene Ontology Annotation (GOA) database. Annotations are sourced by merging \texttt{train\_terms.tsv} from the CAFA-6 challenge with experimentally curated GAF (Gene Association File) records. Following standard practice, the dataset is filtered using the ``no IEA'' (no Inferred from Electronic Annotation) criterion to ensure high-quality, experimentally validated annotations. Sequences are further filtered to retain only those with lengths between 30 and 2{,}000 amino acids, and duplicate sequences are removed. The final processed dataset contains \textbf{46{,}978 proteins} annotated across approximately 400 target GO terms spanning the Molecular Function (MF), Biological Process (BP), and Cellular Component (CC) aspects. The dataset is split into training (70\%, $n = 32{,}884$), validation (15\%, $n = 7{,}046$), and test (15\%, $n = 7{,}048$) partitions using a stratified random split to preserve label distribution.

\subsection{Training Configuration}
The model is trained using Binary Cross-Entropy with Logits Loss (\texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss}), which provides stable gradient flow for the large-scale multi-label setting with approximately 400 output classes. We employ a cosine learning rate scheduler with linear warmup (10\% of training steps), gradient clipping (max norm = 1.0), and weight decay ($\lambda = 0.01$). The ESM2-t12-35M backbone is kept fully frozen to leverage pre-trained evolutionary representations while enabling efficient training on the full 46K-protein dataset. Only the classification heads and fusion layers are trained. Dropout is set to $p = 0.3$ throughout. The optimal threshold for binarization is determined by maximizing $F_{\max}$ on the validation set.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We evaluate HUCAP using both discriminative and calibration metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$F_{\max}$}: Maximum protein-centric F-score across all thresholds~\cite{radivojac2013}---the primary CAFA evaluation metric.
    \item \textbf{AUPRC}: Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve, robust to class imbalance.
    \item \textbf{ECE}: Expected Calibration Error, measuring the gap between predicted confidence and actual accuracy~\cite{guo2017}.
    \item \textbf{Brier Score}: Mean squared error between predicted probabilities and binary outcomes.
    \item \textbf{Micro~F1 / Macro~F1}: Standard multi-label classification metrics assessing per-sample and per-class performance, respectively.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare HUCAP against four ablated configurations to isolate the contribution of each component, and additionally position HUCAP relative to existing protein function prediction methods:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No Uncertainty}: Raw model without any uncertainty or calibration modifications.
    \item \textbf{Entropy-Only}: Shannon entropy-based confidence adjustment without calibration.
    \item \textbf{Smooth Scaling}: Hybrid entropy + $k$-mer smooth scaling (Eq.~\ref{eq:smooth}).
    \item \textbf{MC Dropout}: Monte Carlo Dropout~\cite{gal2016} with $N=10$ stochastic passes.
    \item \textbf{Temp-Calibrated (HUCAP)}: Full pipeline with Temperature Scaling ($T=1.35$) + Isotonic Regression.
\end{enumerate}

The $F_{\max}$ metric, which is computed over the threshold-optimized protein-centric F-score, remained stable, indicating that the calibration and ranking behavior are consistent across dataset scales. Coverage also remained at 90.1\%, confirming that the rejection threshold generalizes well.

\section{Explainability and Interpretability}
%==============================================================================

HUCAP implements a multi-layered explainability framework designed to satisfy the transparency requirements of clinical and functional genomics research.

\subsection{Confidence Decomposition}
Each prediction is accompanied by a structured breakdown following Eq.~\ref{eq:decomp}, producing four explainable stages: (1)~base model probability from the ESM2 classifier head, (2)~calibration adjustment from Temperature Scaling and Isotonic Regression, (3)~entropy penalty reflecting global compositional bias, and (4)~$k$-mer diversity assessment reflecting local sequential repetitiveness. This decomposition enables researchers to trace exactly how much each factor contributed to the final confidence score.

\textbf{Illustrative example.} Consider a query protein with moderate compositional bias (entropy = 3.6 bits). The decomposition proceeds as follows: base model probability $p_{\text{base}} = 0.72$; calibration adjustment reduces confidence by 0.08 ($p_{\text{cal}} = 0.64$); entropy penalty of 0.12 is applied due to low compositional diversity; $k$-mer diversity penalty contributes an additional 0.02. The final UAC score is $0.72 - 0.08 - 0.12 - 0.02 = 0.50$. This step-by-step trace allows biologists to identify that entropy---not model uncertainty---is the primary reason for reduced confidence, guiding them to investigate whether the query protein genuinely contains low-complexity regions.

\subsection{Practical Utility of Explanations}
The decomposition framework serves three concrete purposes in practice: (i)~\textit{Prediction debugging}: researchers can determine whether a low-confidence score results from genuine biological ambiguity or from a compositional artifact, avoiding unnecessary experimental follow-up on unreliable predictions; (ii)~\textit{Unreliable sequence identification}: sequences flagged as \texttt{HIGH} uncertainty with large entropy penalties are candidates for manual curation or alternative annotation methods such as homology search; (iii)~\textit{Experimental guidance}: counterfactual explanations (e.g., ``if the sequence entropy increased above 3.85 bits, the penalty would be removed, increasing confidence by approximately 15\%'') help researchers assess whether mutagenesis or domain truncation experiments could yield more reliable functional annotations.

\subsection{Counterfactual Explanations}
For each non-structured prediction, HUCAP generates counterfactual statements indicating \textit{what would need to change} for the prediction to be more reliable. These counterfactuals provide actionable guidance for experimental design and are presented alongside the decomposition in the web interface.

\subsection{Token-Level Attribution Maps}
The attention-based residue importance scores (Section III-H) are visualized as interactive heatmaps in the web interface, enabling researchers to identify which residues most influenced the prediction. This local explainability complements the global confidence decomposition, offering both ``why this confidence'' and ``which residues matter'' perspectives.

\section{Discussion}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Why Calibration Matters for Protein Prediction}
Our results demonstrate that uncalibrated protein function prediction models exhibit ECE values exceeding 0.26, meaning predictions are off by over 26 percentage points on average. In a clinical setting where predicted protein functions guide drug target selection, such miscalibrated confidence could lead to costly experimental failures. By reducing ECE to 0.056, HUCAP ensures that a prediction with 80\% confidence is correct approximately 80\% of the time.

\subsection{Impact of Dataset Scaling}
The transition from the 3K-protein exploratory subset to the full 46K dataset produced a $32\times$ improvement in Macro~F1 (0.013 $\to$ 0.42). This improvement is primarily attributed to the increased per-class sample count: on the 3K subset, the majority of the 400 GO terms had fewer than five positive training examples, rendering per-class learning infeasible. The full dataset provides sufficient representation for underrepresented GO terms, substantially reducing class imbalance and enabling the model to learn discriminative features even for rare functional annotations. This result underscores the importance of dataset scale in multi-label protein function prediction and validates the investment in a comprehensive data integration pipeline.

\subsection{The Role of Input-Level Uncertainty}
The strong entropy-confidence correlation ($r = 0.86$, $p < 10^{-99}$) empirically validates our hypothesis that compositional diversity of the input sequence is a reliable proxy for epistemic uncertainty. This is grounded in the observation that ESM2's pre-training distribution (UniRef50) is biased toward globular, well-structured proteins; low-complexity sequences are underrepresented and therefore poorly modeled.

\subsection{Limitations}
We acknowledge several limitations of this work:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset scale}: While 46{,}978 proteins represents a significant improvement over earlier subsets, this is still a subset of the full CAFA-scale benchmark, which includes millions of protein-GO term associations. Training at that scale was not feasible within our computational budget.
    \item \textbf{Frozen backbone}: The ESM2-t12-35M encoder is kept fully frozen during training. While this is computationally efficient and leverages high-quality pre-trained representations, partial or full fine-tuning of the encoder may yield further gains in discriminative performance.
    \item \textbf{PSSM approximations}: The current deployment uses pseudo-PSSM profiles computed from amino acid frequency statistics rather than full PSI-BLAST profiles, which limits the evolutionary information available to the fusion module.
    \item \textbf{Heuristic threshold selection}: The $k$-mer diversity parameter ($k=3$) and the rejection threshold ($\theta_r = 0.45$) were selected empirically and may benefit from systematic optimization, potentially via Bayesian hyperparameter search.
    \item \textbf{Fixed rejection criterion}: The current rejection system uses a fixed threshold; an adaptive, per-GO-aspect threshold may improve rejection precision for terms with varying difficulty.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work}
Several directions merit investigation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Backbone fine-tuning}: Selectively unfreezing the last $k$ transformer layers of ESM2, or employing LoRA-based parameter-efficient fine-tuning, could improve discriminative performance while maintaining computational feasibility.
    \item \textbf{Structural feature integration}: Incorporating predicted structural features from AlphaFold2~\cite{jumper2021} (e.g., pLDDT scores, contact maps) as additional inputs to the fusion module may capture function-relevant structural signals not present in sequence-only representations.
    \item \textbf{Evidential uncertainty}: Replacing the current entropy-based proxy with evidential deep learning~\cite{sensoy2018} would provide closed-form aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty estimates directly from the model output.
    \item \textbf{Human-in-the-loop validation}: Integrating expert biologist feedback into the rejection and re-ranking system could improve precision in safety-critical annotation scenarios.
    \item \textbf{Extended applications}: Applying HUCAP to protein--protein interaction prediction, variant effect prediction, and enzyme commission number classification tasks could demonstrate its generalizability beyond GO annotation.
\end{itemize}

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We presented HUCAP, a hybrid uncertainty-calibrated framework for protein function prediction that addresses a critical gap in the reliability of deep learning-based biological predictions. Through the integration of Shannon entropy and $k$-mer diversity for input-level uncertainty estimation, Temperature Scaling and Isotonic Regression for post-hoc calibration, a novel UAC metric for uncertainty-aware ranking, and confidence-based rejection with quantitative explainability, HUCAP reduces Expected Calibration Error by 78.6\% (ECE: $0.2614 \to 0.0560$) while maintaining identical discriminative performance ($F_{\max} = 0.3943$). On the full 46{,}978-protein dataset, HUCAP achieves Micro~F1 = 0.58 and Macro~F1 = 0.42, representing a $32\times$ improvement in Macro~F1 over the initial 3K-protein subset. Statistical tests confirm significance at $p < 10^{-99}$ with large effect sizes (Cohen's $d = 5.40$). The framework achieves 100\% detection rate for pathological low-complexity inputs and maintains 90.1\% prediction coverage at the rejection threshold. This work was extended from a prototype trained on 3{,}319 proteins to a fully scalable system evaluated on 46{,}978 proteins, substantially improving generalization and reliability. To the best of our knowledge, HUCAP is the first framework to jointly and systematically address uncertainty estimation, calibration, confidence rejection, and explainable decomposition for multi-aspect protein function prediction.
